1. ChatGPT
Use it for: Writing content, analyzing documents, and making videos.
URL: https://chatgpt.com/
ChatGPT is an easy-to-use AI assistant that helps with writing, research analysis, brainstorming ideas, and problem-solving. The tool is particularly good at document analysis – through the chat interface, you can upload PDFs, spreadsheets, screenshots, and other files, and ask it to summarize insights or highlight trends in data.
ChatGPT also connects easily to other tools from OpenAI (its parent company), for example, Sora 2 which is an AI video generating tool. Sora 2 can make cinematic, story-driven clips and is one of the more advanced narrative video creation platforms available. ChatGPT’s integration with tools like Sora 2 means you can use it for both creative and administrative tasks, as well as professional and personal projects.
You can use ChatGPT with a free account, but there are some usage limits. Paid accounts start at $8 per month.
2. Gemini
Use it for: Search queries, compiling complex research, and making images.
URL: https://gemini.google.com/
Gemini is the AI assistant from Google. It’s one of the most popular AI tools because of its multiple uses, including to help with research, writing, data analysis, and multimedia creation.
Gemini can handle large amounts of text at once, so if you’re uploading long papers or dense documents, you can ask detailed questions about them and the tool can provide accurate responses that highlight where information from the sources has been pulled.
The Deep Research version of Gemini breaks down complex research tasks and creates interactive reports, too. It can even provide reasoning within its customized research reports.
For more creative uses, Gemini has the fun-named Nano Banana as its AI image generator and editor, capable of precise edits, background swaps, object additions, and full scene transformations.
Meanwhile, the audio overview feature of Gemini converts documents into short, podcast-style summaries, so you can package your content in more diverse ways. Pretty cool, right?
There’s a free version of Gemini, but most of the advanced features are in the paid plans, which start from $7.99 per month.
3. Veo
Use it for: Creating AI videos from text or with image prompts.
URL: https://labs.google/fx/tools/flow
Veo creates short, cinematic video clips from written or visual prompts. You can either upload an image or describe a scene and get a video with realistic lighting, natural motion, and synchronized sound.
What makes Veo stand out is that its videos have smooth camera movement and objects aren’t distorted (as is the case for many other AI video generators). The tool is great for building trailers, branded visuals, and mood-driven scenes where realism and atmosphere matter. For businesses, Veo can produce videos for product ads, social media teasers, films, and games.
You can access Veo for free, or paid plans begin from $19.99 per month and offer higher quality video production.
4. Claude
Use it for: Writing code and automating processes.
URL: https://claude.ai/
Claude is an AI assistant from Anthropic, and is the AI tool of choice for coding and structured reasoning. It’s effective for writing and researching, but is best at supporting development workflows – that’s why its most popular use cases are software development, debugging, refactoring, technical writing, and learning to code.
Claude’s strength is writing clean code – typically with fewer logic errors than other AI tools – and being able to explain it in plain English. It can also work directly with large codebases and real projects, instead of just generating snippets of code in the chat.
Claude Cowork is available in the app version of Claude, and can carry out tasks across your computer files and tools without you needing to write any code. For people who don’t know how to code, this capability lets you easily set up and automate processes.
Like other AI tools, there’s a free version of Claude, but more sophisticated features come with the paid plans, starting at $17 per month.
5. Grok
Use it for: Fact checking and generating images and videos.
URL: https://grok.com/
Grok is mostly used as an AI assistant on the social media platform X (formerly Twitter). It’s an AI tool that fact-checks posts and can join online debates to add contextual information or highlight when claims are incorrect or unsupported by evidence.
To use Grok, you tag ‘@Grok’ on X posts. The tool then writes and publishes replies in real time and often cites sources to back up what it’s shared. For many people, Grok not only helps reduce misinformation, it makes AI part of everyday conversations on big platforms.
Beyond X, Grok has features like ‘Think’ and ‘Deep Search’ to answer queries and write long-form responses. Plus, it can generate images for content ranging from memes to branding components, while Grok Imagine can support fast AI video creation, mostly for short, high-impact clips.
Grok’s tiered plans start from free and increase to $300 per year.
6. NotebookLM
Use it for: Answering questions using your own documents.
URL: https://notebooklm.google/
If you want to upload, organize, and summarize large amounts of information, NotebookLM is the AI tool for you. You can input PDFs, course materials, notes, and other files and the tool will pull key ideas into clear, concise overviews, as well as answer any questions you have about the files’ content. It’s a brilliant tool to close any knowledge gaps you might have about a subject.
An added bonus is that NotebookLM has an audio summary feature that turns files’ content into a podcast-style recap, so you can listen to the information in a more engaging format.
Whether studying for exams, reviewing research papers, summarizing documents or getting up to speed on dense topics, NotebookLM is a savvy AI addition.
You can use Notebook LM for free, or tiered pricing starts at $8.99 per month.
7. Lovable
Use it for: Building simple web apps from simple prompts.
URL: https://lovable.dev/
Loveable is the AI tool for building apps and websites – even if you’re not a coder or designer. Instead of using code, you can use basic tools and interactive widgets to put an app or website together.
It’s handy for marketers and entrepreneurs who may not have deep technical knowledge but want to create an online presence or an app. It also saves users time because of its intuitive, straightforward set up.
Loveable isn’t for enterprise-grade products, but it’s a powerful starting point for people who are working independently or without development teams. For example, it’s ideal for personal portfolio sites, SaaS-style MVPs, internal tools, and quick prototypes for testing.
Loveable is free, or paid access starts from $25 per month.
8. ElevenLabs
Use it for: Generating realistic voices, text-to-speech, and voice agents.
ElevenLabs is probably the AI tool that produces the most realistic AI voices from text. It’s commonly used for audiobooks, podcast narration, YouTube voiceovers, customer support, and much more. You just enter what you want the audio to say, choose from different languages, accents, and speakers, and use voice tags like ‘whispers’, ‘sarcastic’, and ‘laughs’ to control the delivery.
All the voices sound natural, and there’s the option to clone voices with impressive accuracy. Once trained, your cloned voice can read any text you submit – meaning you can scale audio consistently and quickly.
ElevenLabs has Voice Agents too, which are conversational voice bots you can speak to in real time. These can be integrated into apps, customer support systems, call centers, or workflows.
There’s a free version of ElevenLabs, while paid accounts range from $5 to $99 per month.
9. Synthesia
Use it for: Creating business videos and video agents from scripts and documents.
URL: https://www.synthesia.io/
Synthesia is a one-stop AI tool for business videos and video agents. With it, you can turn prompts, scripts, documents, presentations, and webpages into interactive videos. You can even select and customize an AI avatar to lead the video, naturally guiding viewers through the content.
It’s a beneficial tool if you’re working on employee training, onboarding, product explainers, internal communications, multilingual content, and interactive role play learning.
Videos from Synthesia don’t require any filming or editing, so non-technical teams can produce high-quality videos in minutes. For the more creative types, Synthesia includes an ‘AI Playground’ where you get free access to Veo 3 and Sora 2 to experiment making B-roll for videos.
There are additionally Video Agents in the tool that let viewers interact with videos in real time, which makes content more engaging and is useful for training and simulation videos.
The Basic plan from Synthesia is free. Pricing begins at $18 per month.
10. Higgsfield
Use it for: Making inspired dynamic AI images and videos.
Higgsfield is an AI tool for creating AI images and videos. It’s also my main creative workspace, as I use it for image and video generation. With the tool, I can work with a wide range of top-tier models inside one clean, professional interface, without constantly switching platforms. For me, the overall experience feels designed for real production work rather than casual experimentation.
I also love the combination of unlimited image generation with Higgsfield, and how quickly new models appear inside the tool. I can test new generators almost immediately, reuse reference images, move from image generation to video, and iterate inside the same ecosystem. This saves me a lot of time when I work on short films, advertising content, and creative concepts.
In my opinion, Higgsfield AI is one of the most balanced all-in-one platforms available today for serious creative work. It also helps that the huge catalogue of trending AI-generated content from its community keeps me inspired and keeps my content up-to-date.
Higgsfield is one of the few AI tools that doesn’t have a free plan. The basic account costs $9 per month.
11. Gamma
Use it for: Converting notes into presentations, documents, and webpages.
URL: https://gamma.app/
Gamma is an efficiency-lovers’ dream AI tool. It converts notes into decks, documents, and webpages. To use it, you enter your notes, describe the size and scope of your preferred format, and voila, you’ll have it generated in seconds. You can then edit the deck, document or webpage copy, and export the content if you’ve generated a PowerPoint (.pptx).
The decks from Gamma are especially clean and stylish, and come with the option of impressive AI-generated visuals. And, if you’re someone who has to regularly give presentations, you can use Gamma to quickly format professional-looking slides, while you concentrate on refining your ideas.
Gamma is available with a free plan, Plus plan ($9 per month), Pro plan ($18 per month) or Ultra plan ($90).
12. Fathom
Use it for: Recording meetings and creating summaries and action items.
You’ve probably seen Fathom pop up during your work calls. It’s an AI notetaker that joins Zoom or Microsoft Teams meetings and records the conversation, and it’s one of the most genuinely useful tools I use day-to-day.
Fathom makes a full transcript of the call, and afterward produces a structured summary. If I need to be reminded of an action item after a call or double-check what someone said, Fathom has me covered. The notes are always accurate and well-organized in my experience, and I think it captures questions and action items well.
For busy professionals, people with packed call calendars, or just those who love to chat, Fathom is one of the top transcript AI tools – sometimes I wonder how I ever worked without it.
Fathom is free (and says it will be forever) but paid plans start from $15 per month and have advanced features like CRM sync.

AI is already part of everyday business work, even when there is no formal plan for its secure use. One team member may use ChatGPT to draft content, another may test Claude for research and someone else may use Gemini for analysis. Before long, AI activity is spread across multiple tools, logins, subscriptions and privacy settings.
For businesses and agencies, that scattered setup can create real risk. Client notes, website content, sales data, internal plans and sensitive prompts may move through platforms your team does not fully manage or understand. The problem is not just AI use. It is unmanaged AI use.

The key security risks in AI systems include prompt injection attacks, data poisoning, model inversion and extraction, sensitive data leakage and supply chain vulnerabilities. These risks matter because AI systems do not just process code and infrastructure. They also process prompts, training data, model weights, connected tools and user inputs. This creates attack paths that traditional security controls often miss.
[OWASP](https://genai.owasp.org/llmrisk/llm01-prompt-injection/) now lists prompt injection as a top LLM risk and [NIST’s adversarial ML taxonomy](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ai/NIST.AI.100-2e2025.pdf) covers threats such as poisoning, privacy breaches and supply-chain attacks across the AI lifecycle.
### 1. Prompt injection attacks
Prompt injection attacks happen when an attacker crafts input that manipulates a model into ignoring its original instructions or revealing information it should not expose. In practice, this can mean bypassing safety rules, extracting hidden system prompts or tricking an AI app into taking unsafe actions through connected tools or retrieval systems.
OWASP warns that prompt injection can cause data leakage, unauthorized actions and unsafe outputs and that common techniques like RAG do not fully solve the problem by themselves. Model jailbreaks are a real-world version of this risk, where users try to override safeguards with carefully written prompts.
### 2. Data poisoning
Data poisoning occurs when malicious or low-integrity data is inserted into the training, fine-tuning or retrieval pipeline so the model learns the wrong patterns or produces manipulated results. This can weaken model quality, introduce hidden biases or create backdoors that only activate under certain conditions.
NIST identifies poisoning as a core adversarial ML threat and notes that it can affect multiple learning paradigms, making it a serious concern for organizations training custom models or relying on external datasets.
### 3. Model inversion and extraction
Model inversion and extraction attacks target the model itself. Inversion attacks try to infer sensitive information about training data from model outputs, while extraction attacks aim to reconstruct the model’s behavior, logic or proprietary value through repeated querying. 
For businesses, that can mean privacy harm, loss of intellectual property and competitive risk if a model’s unique behavior is copied or probed too deeply. NIST includes privacy breaches and model-targeted attacks in its adversarial ML taxonomy because these risks can expose both data and the model’s underlying value.
### 4. Sensitive data leakage
Sensitive data leakage happens when prompts, outputs, logs or chat history expose confidential information such as PII, financial details, internal documents or client data. This is one of the most immediate risks for businesses using AI in everyday workflows. 
A well-known real-world example came from [OpenAI’s March 2023 ChatGPT outage](https://openai.com/index/march-20-chatgpt-outage/), when some users could see titles from other users’ chat histories, showing how even limited exposure can undermine privacy and trust.
In business settings, this same category of risk becomes more serious when employees paste customer records, legal content or proprietary material into AI tools without strong privacy controls.
### 5. Supply chain vulnerabilities
Supply chain vulnerabilities affect the broader AI stack, including open-source models, model files, datasets, dependencies, plugins and deployment components. If any part of that chain is compromised organizations may inherit hidden malware, poisoned artifacts, unsafe defaults or manipulated model behavior.
NIST specifically calls out AI supply-chain attacks, including risks tied to model technology suppliers and malicious code added upstream. This is especially important for teams adopting open-source models quickly without verifying provenance, integrity and update hygiene.

Pricing: The Real Math
Pricing in automation tools is deceptive. The sticker price means nothing without understanding what counts as a "task," "operation," or "execution."
How Each Platform Counts
- Zapier tasks: Each action in a Zap that runs successfully counts as one task. A 5-step Zap that runs once = 5 tasks. Trigger steps are free.
- Make operations: Each module (node) that processes data counts as one operation. A 5-module scenario running once = 5 operations. Modules that don't process data (routers, filters) are free.
- n8n executions: Each workflow execution counts as one, regardless of how many nodes it contains. A 20-node workflow that runs once = 1 execution. This is the most generous counting method.

- Custom chains combining multiple LLM calls with logic

The combination of self-hosting + local LLM support + MCP means you can build enterprise-grade AI automation that runs entirely on your own hardware. If you're processing sensitive data — medical records, financial documents, legal contracts — this matters enormously.

What you can build:

- No built-in vector store integration
- No local LLM support (cloud-only)
- No MCP support
- Limited code execution makes complex prompt engineering harder
Make works well when you need AI as a step in a larger automation — "when a new email arrives, use GPT-4o to classify it, then route it to the right Slack channel." It's less suited for building autonomous agents that need to reason over multiple steps.
Zapier: AI for Everyone
Zapier's AI strategy focuses on accessibility. Their Copilot builds automations from natural language descriptions, and their chatbot builder lets non-technical users create AI-powered conversational interfaces.
What you can build:
- AI chatbots with custom knowledge bases (up to 20 chatbots on Advanced plan)
- Natural language workflow creation ("When I get an email from a client, summarize it and create a Trello card")
- AI-powered fields that transform data inline
- Code by Zapier for custom logic (JavaScript/Python)
The AI Copilot: Zapier's headline feature. Describe what you want in plain English, and Copilot generates the Zap. It's impressive for simple workflows but struggles with complex multi-step logic. Think of it as AI-assisted setup rather than AI-powered execution.
Limitations:
- No agent loops or autonomous reasoning
- Limited LLM provider support (OpenAI-heavy)
- No self-hosting, no local models
- Chatbot builder is separate from workflow engine
- AI features are add-ons with their own pricing tiers
Zapier's approach is "AI as a feature" rather than "AI as architecture." It's the right choice for teams that want AI to enhance existing automations without fundamentally rethinking their workflow design.

- Zapier: 7,000+ app integrations. The largest ecosystem by far. If a SaaS tool exists, Zapier probably connects to it. This is Zapier's unassailable moat.
- Make: 1,800+ apps. Covers all major tools and most niche ones. Fewer than Zapier but growing fast, and the data mapping capabilities are superior.
- n8n: 422+ community-built nodes. The smallest catalog, but it includes all major services. The HTTP Request node covers anything without a dedicated integration. Plus, n8n's code execution nodes let you call any API or run any script.
For most teams, all three cover the apps they actually use. The integration count matters most when you need obscure or industry-specific tools — that's where Zapier's breadth shines.
Complexity and Learning Curve
Zapier: 15 Minutes to Your First Zap
Zapier is the tool your marketing manager can use without training. The step-by-step builder holds your hand. Templates cover common use cases. The learning curve is essentially flat for simple automations.
The downside: when you need something complex — conditional branching, error handling, data transformation, loops — Zapier's simplicity becomes a constraint. You end up building workarounds (multiple Zaps chained together) that are harder to maintain than just using a more powerful tool.
Make: An Hour to Get Comfortable
Make's visual scenario builder is more powerful than Zapier's step editor. You see the entire flow as a diagram. Data mapping is explicit — you see exactly which fields flow where. Branching, error handling, and iteration are first-class features.
The trade-off: you need to understand how data flows through modules. Non-technical users can learn it, but there's a genuine 2-3 hour investment before you're comfortable building multi-step scenarios.
n8n: A Weekend to Get Productive
n8n is a developer tool that happens to have a visual UI. It looks approachable, but the real power comes from understanding JavaScript expressions, JSON data structures, and API concepts. If you can read a cURL command, you'll love n8n. If you can't, you'll be frustrated.
The workflow editor is node-based — similar to Make but with more options per node. The Code node lets you write arbitrary JavaScript or Python, which is either liberating or terrifying depending on your background.
For teams already building coding agents or working with LLMs programmatically, n8n feels natural. The mental model transfers directly.
